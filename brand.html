Polymer Clay Brands

When getting started with Polymer Clay, you might notice the abundance of different polymer clay types and brands out that are out there. It might get a little overwhelming trying to figure out what to get started with, considering that each polymer clay brand and type all work differently from each other. The goal of this page is to touch on some of the more common brands and types.

Sculpey Original

It is a very soft clay that is easy to work with. It comes in large quantities with white, terra cotta, and granite colors. However, its over-softness is its downside, making it mushy and brittle/weak after baking.

Sculpey Premo

It's a very versatile and a very beloved clay in the Polymer Clay community. It's easy to condition, not too soft or too hard, and is very flexible/durable after baking. It has a bunch of nice primary colors that mix pretty well with other colors to get a specific pallete. It is what most people use, given how efficient its results are.

Sculpey III

It's much stronger than original Sculpey but can be pretty brittle after baking. It is pretty easy to work with, but is also regarded as too soft, making it hard to make sharp details. Its biggest advantage is the colors. It comes in a huge variety of different colors and shades. You can mix it with stronger clays and still retain the color that you want.

Super Sculpey

Also comes in large quantities like Original Sculpey with a light beige color. It's pretty translucent but darkens after baking. It bakes pretty hard and firm, which makes it the best for sculptures or dolls.

Sculpey Souffle

It's a very flexible and strong clay while also being soft and easy to handle. However, it doesn't have that many bright color options, and mixing different colors can give some unpredictable results. It is regarded as an all-around solid clay.

Fimo Professional

Fimo Professional is also a highly regarded clay like Premo, but is decently hard to condition as it comes a little hard out of the package. It's pretty easy to work with after conditioning, versatile, and bakes very durable. Its colors are based on a true primary color palette, making it perfect for being able to mix colors based on color theory to get a specific color.

Fimo Soft

As the name suggests, it's pretty easy to work with and strong after baking. However, its softness can cause problems when making fine details. It also has a bit of a lower shelf life than other clays. It also has a bunch of different colors, like Sculpey III.

Fimo Leather

A textured polymer clay that makes it look, feel and act like leather which makes it pretty flexible. It is also an incredibly difficult clay to condition, but is incredibly strong after baking. It has an good amount of decent colors.

Pardo Polymer Clay

A German brand that has a pretty large spectrum of both regular and shiny colors. It's soft enough to work with but also firm and a little stiff. It also has the clearest translucent clays on the market.

Papa's Clay

More of the newer ones to coming to the polymer clay market. It's pretty good at holding sharp details, is soft but not mushy, and has a huge range of colors. However, it does not do well to be conditioning in a pasta machine and instead will have to be warmed up and kneaded in your hands. There have been changes made to make it condition better with a pasta machine.

Cernit polymer Clay

One of the best translucent clays out there, and it has many different types and colors. It is pretty easy to condition, but it may end up getting too soft. It's also strong after baking.

CosClay

Also, a newer clay that conditions easily due to how soft it is. It's very rubbery and flexible making it easy to bend. It has some good colors and skin colors to make clay-dolls.

Kato PolyClay

Also a clay whose colors are meant to be close to a primary color palette. Its hard to conditioned and stiff to work with. But,it's incribily strong and last long making it good for polymer clay techniques like canning. Though, it has a pretty distinct smell that might turn some people off.
